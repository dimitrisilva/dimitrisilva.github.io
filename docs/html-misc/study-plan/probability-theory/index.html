Measure-Theoretic Probability Theory

This self-study plan is designed for learners seeking a rigorous introduction to measure-theoretic probability theory, drawing from three key textbooks: Jeffrey S. Rosenthal's A First Look at Rigorous Probability Theory (referred to as Rosenthal), Marek Capiński and Ekkehard Kopp's Measure, Integral and Probability (referred to as Capiński & Kopp), and chapters 6 through 9 of Dean Corbae, Maxwell B. Stinchcombe, and Juraj Zeman's An Introduction to Mathematical Analysis for Economic Theory and Econometrics (referred to as Corbae et al.). The plan emphasizes material from Corbae et al. where applicable, while incorporating gentler expositions from the other texts to build foundational understanding. All specified content from the textbooks is covered, with overlaps highlighted to avoid redundancy while ensuring comprehensive exposure. The plan is divided into topic-based modules, each building on the previous to create a logical progression from basic concepts to advanced applications in probability.


Prerequisites from Corbae et al. Chapters 1–5

To understand the content in chapters 6–9 of Corbae et al., learners must be familiar with fundamental mathematical tools from earlier chapters, though these are not part of the core study plan. From Chapter 1, grasp basic logic including statements, implication, quantifiers, and proof techniques (sections 1.1–1.5). From Chapter 2, know set theory basics such as notation, products, relations, functions, equivalence relations, orders, lattices, finite/infinite sets, and the axiom of choice (sections 2.1–2.4, 2.6–2.7, 2.10–2.11). From Chapter 3, understand the real numbers, including rationality, distance, Cauchy sequences, completeness, supremum/infimum, summability, and lim inf/sup (sections 3.1–3.7, 3.9).

From Chapter 4, review finite-dimensional metric spaces, including definitions, discrete spaces, norms in ℝⁿ, completeness, closure/convergence, separability, compactness, continuous functions, and connectedness (sections 4.1–4.8, 4.12). From Chapter 5, focus on convex analysis essentials like geometry of convexity, separation theorems, concave/convex functions, and fixed-point theorems (sections 5.1, 5.3–5.6, 5.11). These concepts provide the analytical foundation for metric and measure spaces; review them as needed for self-paced progress.


Module 1: Motivation and Preliminaries

This module introduces the need for measure theory in probability, covering basic set theory, limitations of Riemann integration, and concepts like countable/uncountable sets and topological properties in ℝ. It establishes why rigorous probability requires measures to handle continuous random variables and non-measurable sets, setting the stage for constructing probability spaces in subsequent modules.

Primary Source. Capiński & Kopp, Chapter 1 (detailed on Riemann limitations and random number selection).

Secondary Sources. Rosenthal, Chapter 1 (concise on various random variables and non-measurable sets); Corbae et al., Chapter 6, Sections 6.1–6.2 (complementary on metric spaces for broader context, emphasizing compactness and continuous functions).

Prerequisite Material. None from the specified texts, but review set theory and real numbers from Corbae et al. prerequisites.

Skippable Concepts/Results. In Capiński & Kopp, skip detailed examples of Riemann integral failures if familiar with calculus; in Rosenthal, skip exercises on paradoxical sets unless interested in foundational issues.

Suggested Exercises. From Capiński & Kopp, Exercise 1.3 (proving properties of countable sets, used in measure extension); from Rosenthal, Exercise 1.2 (constructing non-measurable sets, foundational for later uniqueness theorems).

Study Steps.

  1. Read Capiński & Kopp Chapter 1 for overview of set theory and Riemann limits.
  2. Compare with Rosenthal Chapter 1 for probability-specific motivation.
  3. Skim Corbae et al. Sections 6.1–6.2 for metric preliminaries.
  4. Work suggested exercises to solidify countability.
  5. Note overlaps: All texts emphasize uncountable sets, but Corbae adds metric compactness for later optimization.
Module 2: Metric Spaces

This module explores general metric spaces, including compactness, completeness, continuous functions, and approximations, which are crucial for understanding probability measures on abstract spaces and weak convergence in later modules.

Primary Source. Corbae et al., Chapter 6 (detailed on compact sets, continuous functions, distribution functions, approximation, and Lebesgue measure introduction).

Secondary Sources. Capiński & Kopp, Chapter 1, Sections 1.1.3 (complementary on topology in ℝ); Rosenthal, Appendix A (basic set theory for metric contexts).

Prerequisite Material. Set theory from Module 1; real numbers and finite-dimensional metrics from Corbae prerequisites.

Skippable Concepts/Results. In Corbae et al., skip Section 6.5 (regression as approximation) if not interested in econometric applications; Lemma 6.4.2 (specific approximation bound) not essential for probability.

Suggested Exercises. From Corbae et al., Exercise 6.11.2 (proving completeness of function spaces, used in L^p completeness); from Capiński & Kopp, none specific but review topology exercises.

Study Steps.

  1. Read Corbae et al. Chapter 6 for comprehensive metric theory.
  2. Use Capiński & Kopp Section 1.1.3 for gentler topology intro.
  3. Refer to Rosenthal Appendix A for set axioms if needed.
  4. Prove key theorems like the Metric Completion Theorem.
  5. Work suggested exercises. Overlaps: Corbae emphasizes economic applications like maximum theorem, while others focus on real line topology.
Module 3: Measures and Probability Spaces

This module covers sigma-algebras, measures, outer measures, Lebesgue measure, and probability spaces, providing the foundation for assigning probabilities to events in uncountable sample spaces, leading to integration.

Primary Source. Corbae et al., Chapter 7, Sections 7.1, 7.6, 7.9 (detailed on basics, nonatomic measures, null sets).

Secondary Sources. Capiński & Kopp, Chapter 2 (gentler on outer measure, Lebesgue, Borel sets, probability); Rosenthal, Chapter 2 (concise on probability triples, extension theorem).

Prerequisite Material. Set theory from Module 1; completeness from Module 2.

Skippable Concepts/Results. In Capiński & Kopp, skip Section 2.6.3 (finance applications); in Rosenthal, Theorem 2.3 (extension variants) if time-limited.

Suggested Exercises. From Corbae et al., Exercise 7.11.1 (nonatomic measures existence, used in distributions); from Rosenthal, Exercise 2.3 (constructing measures, foundational for products).

Study Steps.

  1. Start with Capiński & Kopp Chapter 2 for gentle build-up.
  2. Deepen with Corbae Sections 7.1, 7.6.
  3. Use Rosenthal Chapter 2 for extension theorems.
  4. Construct Lebesgue measure step-by-step.
  5. Work exercises. Overlaps: All cover Lebesgue measure, but Corbae emphasizes nonatomic for economics, Rosenthal uniqueness.
Module 4: Measurable Functions

This module introduces measurable functions, their properties, and probability distributions, bridging measures to integration by allowing random variables on measure spaces.

Primary Source. Corbae et al., Chapter 7, Sections 7.3 (good sets, measurability).

Secondary Sources. Capiński & Kopp, Chapter 3 (detailed on Lebesgue-measurable functions, examples, probability); Rosenthal, Chapter 3, Sections 3.1–3.2 (random variables, independence).

Prerequisite Material. Measures from Module 3.

Skippable Concepts/Results. In Capiński & Kopp, skip Section 3.5.5 (finance); Proposition 3.4.3 (minor property).

Suggested Exercises. From Capiński & Kopp, Exercise 3.2 (proving measurability, used in integral definition).

Study Steps.

  1. Read Capiński & Kopp Chapter 3 for basics.
  2. Enhance with Corbae Section 7.3.
  3. Review Rosenthal Sections 3.1–3.2 for probability view.
  4. Practice generating sigma-fields.
  5. Work exercises. Overlaps: Measurability definitions similar, Corbae adds good sets for advanced proofs.
Module 5: Lebesgue Integral and Convergence Theorems

This module defines the Lebesgue integral, monotone/dominated convergence, and relations to Riemann, fundamental for expectations in probability.

Primary Source. Corbae et al., Chapter 7, Sections 7.2, 7.5, 7.12 (limit results, dominated convergence, Lebesgue appendix).

Secondary Sources. Capiński & Kopp, Chapter 4 (integral definition, convergence theorems, probability expectations); Rosenthal, Chapter 4 (expected values, integration connection).

Prerequisite Material. Measurable functions from Module 4.

Skippable Concepts/Results. In Rosenthal, skip Section 4.4 (integration details if familiar); Theorem 4.2.2 (minor variant).

Suggested Exercises. From Corbae et al., Exercise 7.5.1 (uniform integrability, used in limits); from Capiński & Kopp, Exercise 4.3 (integral properties).

Study Steps.

  1. Study Capiński & Kopp Chapter 4 for step-by-step integral build.
  2. Focus on Corbae Sections 7.2, 7.5 for convergence.
  3. Use Rosenthal Chapter 4 for probability applications.
  4. Prove dominated convergence.
  5. Work exercises. Overlaps: Convergence theorems central, Corbae emphasizes uniform integrability.
Module 6: L^p Spaces

This module examines L^p spaces, completeness, norms, and applications, key for advanced probability like moments and regression.

Primary Source. Corbae et al., Chapter 8 (basics, regression, duals, weak convergence).

Secondary Sources. Capiński & Kopp, Chapter 5 (L1, L2, Lp completeness, probability moments); Rosenthal, none direct but Chapter 4 for expectations.

Prerequisite Material. Integral from Module 5.

Skippable Concepts/Results. In Corbae et al., skip Sections 8.6–8.7 (economies/games) if not economic-focused; Proposition 8.3.3 (technical detail).

Suggested Exercises. From Corbae et al., Exercise 8.4.2 (regression proofs, used in estimation); from Capiński & Kopp, Exercise 5.1 (L1 properties).

Study Steps.

  1. Read Corbae Chapter 8 for detailed L^p theory.
  2. Use Capiński & Kopp Chapter 5 for gentler intro.
  3. Connect to Rosenthal expectations.
  4. Prove completeness.
  5. Work exercises. Overlaps: L^p norms, Corbae adds economic uses.
Module 7: Product Measures, Fubini, and Radon-Nikodym

This module covers product measures, Fubini's theorem, Radon-Nikodym, densities, and conditional expectation, essential for joint distributions and conditioning.

Primary Source. Corbae et al., Chapter 7, Sections 7.7, 7.11; Chapter 8, Section 8.5 (products, signed measures, densities).

Secondary Sources. Capiński & Kopp, Chapters 6–7 (products, Fubini, Radon-Nikodym, martingales); Rosenthal, Chapters 6,12,13 (distributions, decompositions, conditional).

Prerequisite Material. L^p from Module 6.

Skippable Concepts/Results. In Capiński & Kopp, skip Section 7.4.3 (finance); Theorem 7.3.4 (advanced variation).

Suggested Exercises. From Corbae et al., Exercise 7.7.1 (Fubini applications); from Rosenthal, Exercise 13.1 (conditional properties).

Study Steps.

  1. Study Capiński & Kopp Chapters 6–7 for basics.
  2. Deepen with Corbae Sections.
  3. Use Rosenthal for probability focus.
  4. Prove Fubini and Radon-Nikodym.
  5. Work exercises. Overlaps: Radon-Nikodym, Corbae emphasizes signed measures.
Module 8: Limit Theorems and Modes of Convergence

This module discusses convergence modes, laws of large numbers, Borel-Cantelli, and central limit theorem, critical for asymptotic probability results.

Primary Source. Corbae et al., Chapter 7, Sections 7.4, 7.10; Chapter 8, Section 8.9 (0-1 laws, Skorohod, weak convergence in L^p).

Secondary Sources. Capiński & Kopp, Chapter 8 (modes, probability convergence, LLN, CLT); Rosenthal, Chapters 5,9,10 (inequalities, limits, weak convergence).

Prerequisite Material. Products from Module 7.

Skippable Concepts/Results. In Rosenthal, skip Section 9.3 (large deviations); Lemma 5.4.1 (minor).

Suggested Exercises. From Capiński & Kopp, Exercise 8.4 (strong LLN); from Rosenthal, Exercise 5.3 (LLN proofs).

Study Steps.

  1. Read Capiński & Kopp Chapter 8 for overview.
  2. Focus on Corbae Sections for advanced.
  3. Use Rosenthal for theorems.
  4. Prove CLT variants.
  5. Work exercises. Overlaps: LLN/CLT, Corbae adds Skorohod.
Module 9: Characteristic Functions and Martingales

This module covers characteristic functions, martingales, and decompositions, useful for distribution identification and stochastic processes.

Primary Source. Corbae et al., Chapter 7, Section 7.8 (nonmeasurable sets, related to decompositions).

Secondary Sources. Capiński & Kopp, Sections 4.7.4, 6.5.4, 7.4.2 (characteristic, martingales); Rosenthal, Chapters 11,14 (characteristic, martingales).

Prerequisite Material. Limits from Module 8.

Skippable Concepts/Results. In Rosenthal, skip Section 14.4 (advanced stopping); Theorem 11.3.1 (generalization).

Suggested Exercises. From Rosenthal, Exercise 11.2 (continuity theorem).

Study Steps.

  1. Read Rosenthal Chapters 11,14 for focus.
  2. Supplement with Capiński & Kopp sections.
  3. Connect to Corbae Section 7.8.
  4. Prove inversion formulas.
  5. Work exercises. Overlaps: Characteristic functions, Corbae on equity applications.
Module 10: Probabilities on Metric Spaces and Advanced Topics

This module addresses probabilities on metric spaces, weak convergence, Riesz representation, regular conditional probabilities, and stochastic processes, culminating in advanced applications.

Primary Source. Corbae et al., Chapter 9 (full chapter on metric probabilities, utility, Polish spaces, CLT proof).

Secondary Sources. Rosenthal, Chapters 10,15 (weak convergence, processes); Capiński & Kopp, none direct.

Prerequisite Material. Characteristic from Module 9.

Skippable Concepts/Results. In Corbae et al., skip Section 9.1 (uncertainty choice) if not economic; Theorem 9.13.1 (nonexistence details).

Suggested Exercises. From Corbae et al., Exercise 9.11.1 (rcp properties).

Study Steps.

  1. Read Corbae Chapter 9 for core.
  2. Use Rosenthal Chapters 10,15 for convergence and processes.
  3. Note economic applications.
  4. Prove Riesz theorem.
  5. Work exercises. Overlaps: Weak convergence, Corbae emphasizes Polish spaces.
