Static Optimization for Economists

This self-study plan is designed for economists seeking a rigorous understanding of static optimization theory, drawing from specified chapters in two key textbooks: A First Course in Optimization Theory by Rangarajan K. Sundaram (1996, hereafter "Sundaram") and An Introduction to Mathematical Analysis for Economic Theory and Econometrics by Dean Corbae, Maxwell B. Stinchcombe, and Juraj Zeman (2009, hereafter "Corbae et al."). The plan covers all material from Sundaram Chapters 1–8 and Corbae et al. Chapters 4–5, with greater emphasis on Corbae et al. where topics overlap, as their treatment often provides deeper economic applications and connections to advanced topics like fixed points and equilibria. The plan is organized into topic-based modules to facilitate logical progression and self-paced learning. Progress by marking modules as "read" to visually track completion.


Prerequisites

To engage effectively with this study plan, learners must be familiar with foundational concepts in logic, set theory, and the real numbers from Corbae et al. Chapters 1–3. From Chapter 1 (Logic), key elements include understanding statements, implications, logical quantifiers (∃ and ∀), and proof techniques such as direct proof, proof by contrapositive, proof by contradiction, and mathematical induction. These are essential for parsing theorems and constructing arguments in optimization proofs. From Chapter 2 (Set Theory), minimally required are basic set notation and operations (unions, intersections, complements), products and relations (including functions and correspondences), equivalence relations, weak and partial orders, and the distinction between finite and infinite sets; concepts like lattices, supermodularity, Tarski's fixed-point theorem, the axiom of choice, and revealed preference are useful but not strictly necessary unless referenced in applications. From Chapter 3 (The Space of Real Numbers), focus on the limitations of rationals, the construction of reals via Cauchy sequences, completeness (every Cauchy sequence converges), supremum and infimum properties, summability of series, lim inf and lim sup, and the completeness axiom's role in ensuring solutions exist. These prerequisites enable comprehension of metric spaces, convergence, and compactness in subsequent modules; without them, proofs involving sequences or bounds may be inaccessible.


Module 1: Metric Spaces, Norms, and Vector Spaces in Finite Dimensions

This module introduces the structure of finite-dimensional spaces like ℝⁿ as metric and normed vector spaces, providing the foundational language for measuring distances, defining convergence, and analyzing optimization problems in multi-dimensional economic settings (e.g., utility maximization with multiple goods). Understanding these concepts is crucial for subsequent modules on completeness, compactness, and continuity, as they underpin the existence and characterization of optima in constrained environments.

Primary Source. Corbae et al., Sections 4.1 (Basic Definitions for Metric Spaces), 4.3 (ℝⁿ as a Normed Vector Space).

Secondary Source. Sundaram, Sections 1.1 (Notation and Preliminary Definitions), 1.2.1–1.2.3 (Sequences, Limits, Subsequences, Cauchy Sequences), 1.3 (Matrices, for vector operations).

Prerequisite Material. Real numbers and distance from Corbae et al. Chapter 3; basic set theory and functions from Chapter 2.

Skippable Concepts/Results. Section 4.2 (Discrete Spaces) in Corbae et al., as it is illustrative but not required for continuous spaces in optimization.

Suggested Exercises. Corbae et al. end-of-chapter problems related to proving norm equivalence (if left as exercise; used in bounds for convergence proofs later).

Study Steps.

  1. Read Corbae et al. Section 4.1; note definitions of metric, metric space, and properties (non-negativity, symmetry, triangle inequality); compare with Euclidean distance from Chapter 3.
  2. Study Corbae et al. Section 4.3; define vector addition, scalar multiplication, inner products, and norms (e.g., Euclidean, sup, L¹); prove that norms induce metrics.
  3. Cross-reference Sundaram Section 1.1 for concise notation on ℝⁿ, inner products, and norms; compute examples of distances in ℝ².
  4. Use Sundaram Section 1.3 to review matrix representations of vectors; practice vector operations.
  5. Prove basic results like norm positivity and triangle inequality; apply to economic examples (e.g., distance between consumption bundles).
Module 2: Completeness, Closure, Convergence, and Separability

Building on metrics, this module explores how sequences behave in finite-dimensional spaces, emphasizing completeness (every Cauchy sequence converges) and closure (limits stay within sets), which ensure solutions to approximation problems exist. These properties are vital for optimization, as they guarantee that iterative economic processes (e.g., price adjustments) converge, laying groundwork for compactness and continuity in ensuring maxima.

Primary Source. Corbae et al., Sections 4.4 (Completeness), 4.5 (Closure, Convergence, and Completeness), 4.6 (Separability).

Secondary Source. Sundaram, Sections 1.2.1–1.2.6 (Sequences, Limits, Subsequences, Cauchy Sequences, Sup/Inf, Lim Sup/Inf).

Prerequisite Material. Metrics and norms from Module 1; Cauchy sequences in ℝ from Corbae et al. Chapter 3.

Skippable Concepts/Results. Advanced separability examples in Section 4.6 if not tied to density arguments later.

Suggested Exercises. Corbae et al. problems on proving completeness of ℝⁿ under various norms (unproven in text; used in contraction mappings).

Study Steps.

  1. Read Corbae et al. Section 4.4; define Cauchy sequences in metric spaces; prove ℝⁿ is complete.
  2. Study Section 4.5; distinguish open/closed sets, closure, and limit points; prove equivalence of convergence definitions.
  3. Explore Section 4.6; understand separability (dense countable subsets); note ℝⁿ is separable.
  4. Review Sundaram Sections 1.2.1–1.2.3 for sequences in ℝⁿ; compute limits and Cauchy checks.
  5. Use Sundaram 1.2.4–1.2.6 for sup/inf in sequences; apply to bounded economic series (e.g., utility levels).
  6. Prove closure properties; test with examples like budget sets.
Module 3: Compactness and Connectedness

This module examines compact sets (closed and bounded in ℝⁿ) and connectedness, which ensure continuous functions attain maxima and preserve intervals under images. Compactness is foundational for existence of optima in economic models (e.g., closed budget sets), connecting prior convergence concepts to practical optimization results.

Primary Source. Corbae et al., Sections 4.7 (Compactness in ℝⁿ), 4.12 (Connectedness).

Secondary Source. Sundaram, Sections 1.2.7–1.2.8 (Open/Closed Sets, Bounded and Compact Sets).

Prerequisite Material. Convergence and closure from Module 2.

Skippable Concepts/Results. Detailed connectedness applications in 4.12 if not central to path-connected sets in optimization.

Suggested Exercises. Corbae et al. problems proving Heine-Borel theorem (unproven; used in Weierstrass).

Study Steps.

  1. Read Corbae et al. Section 4.7; define compactness via open covers; prove Heine-Borel (compact iff closed/bounded in ℝⁿ).
  2. Study Section 4.12; define connected sets; prove intervals in ℝ are connected.
  3. Review Sundaram 1.2.7–1.2.8; identify open/closed balls, compact sets.
  4. Prove compactness implies closure/boundedness; apply to feasible sets in utility max.
  5. Explore connectedness in economic contexts (e.g., connected preference sets).
Module 4: Continuous Functions, Lipschitz/Uniform Continuity, and Correspondences

Focusing on function behavior in metric spaces, this module covers continuity types and correspondences (multi-valued functions), critical for modeling choice sets in economics. These ensure smooth changes in optima, bridging to existence theorems like the maximum theorem.

Primary Source. Corbae et al., Sections 4.8 (Continuous Functions on ℝⁿ), 4.9 (Lipschitz and Uniform Continuity), 4.10 (Correspondences and the Theorem of the Maximum, partial).

Secondary Source. Sundaram, Section 1.4 (Functions: Continuous, Differentiable).

Prerequisite Material. Compactness from Module 3.

Skippable Concepts/Results. Advanced Lipschitz examples in 4.9 if not needed for contractions.

Suggested Exercises. Corbae et al. problems on uniform continuity over compact sets (unproven; used in maxima).

Study Steps.

  1. Read Corbae et al. Section 4.8; define continuity at points and globally; prove continuous images of compact sets are compact.
  2. Study 4.9; distinguish Lipschitz (bounded derivative) and uniform continuity; prove compactness implies uniform continuity.
  3. Introduce correspondences in 4.10; note upper/lower semicontinuity.
  4. Review Sundaram 1.4.1; compare continuity definitions.
  5. Prove preservation of compactness/connectedness; apply to demand functions.
Module 5: Existence of Optima and the Theorem of the Maximum

This module addresses when optimization problems have solutions, using Weierstrass and Berge's maximum theorem for parametric families. Essential for economic theory, it ensures equilibria exist, connecting continuity and compactness to practical optima identification.

Primary Source. Corbae et al., Section 4.10 (Correspondences and the Theorem of the Maximum).

Secondary Source. Sundaram, Chapter 3 (Existence of Solutions: The Weierstrass Theorem), Section 2.1–2.3 (Optimization Problems and Examples).

Prerequisite Material. Continuity and correspondences from Module 4; compactness from Module 3.

Skippable Concepts/Results. None; core for plan.

Suggested Exercises. Sundaram exercises on Weierstrass applications (unproven examples used in constraints).

Study Steps.

  1. Read Corbae et al. 4.10; prove theorem of the maximum for correspondences.
  2. Study Sundaram Chapter 3; prove Weierstrass (continuous function on compact set attains max/min).
  3. Review Sundaram 2.1–2.3; formulate economic problems (e.g., utility max).
  4. Apply to parametric changes; prove argmax is upper semicontinuous.
  5. Solve examples like profit max under compact inputs.
Module 6: Contraction Mappings and Fixed-Point Theorems

Exploring contractions for uniqueness and fixed points for equilibria, this module applies to dynamic models but focuses on static uses (e.g., Nash). It builds on metrics for iterative solution convergence.

Primary Source. Corbae et al., Section 4.11 (Banach's Contraction Mapping Theorem), Sections 5.11–5.12 (Fixed-Point Theorems and Applications).

Secondary Source. None directly overlapping in specified chapters.

Prerequisite Material. Metrics from Module 1; completeness from Module 2.

Skippable Concepts/Results. Section 5.12 (Nash/Perfect Equilibria) if focusing solely on optimization, not games.

Suggested Exercises. Corbae et al. problems proving uniqueness via contractions (unproven; used in equilibria).

Study Steps.

  1. Read Corbae et al. 4.11; define contractions; prove Banach theorem (unique fixed point in complete spaces).
  2. Study 5.11; apply Brouwer/Schauder to general equilibrium.
  3. Explore 5.12; see Kakutani for Nash.
  4. Prove fixed-point existence; apply to market clearing.
  5. Iterate contractions numerically for economic examples.
Module 7: Convex Sets, Geometry, and Boundary Issues

This module delves into convex sets' geometry, crucial for optimization as they ensure unique optima and enable separation. It forms the basis for duality and constraints.

Primary Source. Corbae et al., Sections 5.1 (Basic Geometry of Convexity), 5.5 (Boundary Issues).

Secondary Source. Sundaram, Sections 1.2.9 (Convex Combinations and Sets), 7.1 (Convexity Defined).

Prerequisite Material. Vector spaces from Module 1.

Skippable Concepts/Results. Detailed boundary examples in 5.5 if not needed for separation.

Suggested Exercises. Corbae et al. problems on convex hulls (unproven; used in separation).

Study Steps.

  1. Read Corbae et al. 5.1; define convex sets, combinations, hulls.
  2. Study 5.5; explore relative interiors, boundaries.
  3. Review Sundaram 1.2.9 and 7.1; prove convexity preservation.
  4. Construct examples (e.g., budget sets).
  5. Prove Carathéodory's theorem if applicable.
Module 8: Separation Theorems, Dual Spaces, and Duality

Covering separation of convex sets and dual spaces, this module supports welfare theorems and duality in optimization, linking geometry to economic interpretations.

Primary Source. Corbae et al., Sections 5.2 (Dual Space of ℝⁿ), 5.3 (Three Degrees of Convex Separation), 5.4 (Strong Separation and Neoclassical Duality), 5.7 (Separation and Hahn-Banach Theorem).

Secondary Source. Sundaram, Section 1.6.1 (Separation Theorems).

Prerequisite Material. Convex sets from Module 7.

Skippable Concepts/Results. Advanced Hahn-Banach extensions in 5.7 if not core.

Suggested Exercises. Corbae et al. problems proving supporting hyperplanes (unproven; used in KT).

Study Steps.

  1. Read Corbae et al. 5.2; define dual spaces, linear functionals.
  2. Study 5.3–5.4; prove weak/strong separation; apply to duality (profit/utility).
  3. Explore 5.7; extend to Hahn-Banach.
  4. Review Sundaram 1.6.1; compare separation statements.
  5. Apply to welfare theorems.
Module 9: Concave and Convex Functions, Differentiability

This module studies concave/convex functions' properties, including differentiability, essential for sufficient optimality conditions and economic assumptions like diminishing returns.

Primary Source. Corbae et al., Sections 5.6 (Concave and Convex Functions), 5.10 (Differentiability and Concavity).

Secondary Source. Sundaram, Sections 7.1–7.2 (Concave/Convex Functions, Implications).

Prerequisite Material. Continuity from Module 4; convexity from Module 7.

Skippable Concepts/Results. None; central.

Suggested Exercises. Corbae et al. problems on Hessian characterizations (unproven; used in second-order conditions).

Study Steps.

  1. Read Corbae et al. 5.6; define concave/convex; prove Jensen's inequality.
  2. Study 5.10; link differentiability to concavity via gradients/Hessians.
  3. Review Sundaram 7.1–7.2; prove continuity and derivative properties.
  4. Classify economic functions (e.g., Cobb-Douglas).
  5. Prove second-derivative tests.
Module 10: Unconstrained Optimization

Examining optima without constraints, this module uses first- and second-order conditions, foundational for interior solutions in economic models like profit maximization.

Primary Source. Corbae et al., implicit in 5.6 and 5.10 (via concavity for optima).

Secondary Source. Sundaram, Chapter 4 (Unconstrained Optima).

Prerequisite Material. Differentiability from Module 9.

Skippable Concepts/Results. None.

Suggested Exercises. Sundaram exercises on second-order failures (unproven; illustrates limits).

Study Steps.

  1. Read Sundaram Chapter 4; derive FOCs (gradient zero), SOCs (Hessian negative semidefinite).
  2. Cross-reference Corbae 5.6/5.10 for convexity implications.
  3. Prove necessity/sufficiency.
  4. Solve examples (e.g., cost min interior).
  5. Analyze failures without convexity.
Module 11: Equality-Constrained Optimization and Lagrange Multipliers

This module introduces Lagrange for equality constraints, interpreting multipliers as shadow prices, key for resource allocation in economics.

Primary Source. Corbae et al., Section 5.9 (Interpreting Lagrange Multipliers).

Secondary Source. Sundaram, Chapter 5 (Equality Constraints and the Theorem of Lagrange).

Prerequisite Material. Unconstrained from Module 10.

Skippable Concepts/Results. None.

Suggested Exercises. Sundaram exercises on CQ failures (unproven; shows when method fails).

Study Steps.

  1. Read Sundaram Chapter 5; state Lagrange theorem, constraint qualification.
  2. Study Corbae 5.9; interpret multipliers economically.
  3. Derive FOCs; prove theorem.
  4. Apply to utility max with budget.
  5. Check SOCs.
Module 12: Inequality-Constrained Optimization and Kuhn-Tucker Theorem

Covering KT for inequalities, this module handles binding/non-binding constraints, essential for non-negativity in economic models.

Primary Source. Corbae et al., Section 5.8 (Separation and Kuhn-Tucker Theorem).

Secondary Source. Sundaram, Chapter 6 (Inequality Constraints and the Theorem of Kuhn and Tucker).

Prerequisite Material. Lagrange from Module 11; separation from Module 8.

Skippable Concepts/Results. Mixed constraints in Sundaram 6.4 if focusing on pure inequalities.

Suggested Exercises. Sundaram exercises on slack multipliers (unproven; used in interpretations).

Study Steps.

  1. Read Sundaram Chapter 6; state KT theorem, CQ, complementarity.
  2. Study Corbae 5.8; link to separation.
  3. Derive KKT conditions; prove.
  4. Solve portfolio choice with non-negativity.
  5. Analyze failures.
Module 13: Convexity and Quasi-Convexity in Optimization

This module applies convexity/quasi-convexity for sufficient conditions, ensuring global optima in concave problems like utility maximization.

Primary Source. Corbae et al., implicit in 5.6/5.10 (concavity for optima).

Secondary Source. Sundaram, Chapters 7 (Convex Structures) and 8 (Quasi-Convexity).

Prerequisite Material. Functions from Module 9; KT from Module 12.

Skippable Concepts/Results. Sundaram 8.2 quasi as generalization if time-limited.

Suggested Exercises. Sundaram exercises on quasi-convexity tests (unproven; extends convexity).

Study Steps.

  1. Read Sundaram Chapter 7; apply convexity to unconstrained/KT.
  2. Study Chapter 8; define quasi-concave/convex; prove FOCs sufficient.
  3. Cross-reference Corbae 5.6/5.10 for differentiability.
  4. Prove global optima under concavity.
  5. Apply to producer theory.
